## Why You Actually Need a 2.5G Switch in 2026

Sky, Virgin Media, and full-fibre providers now offer multi-gigabit broadband — 1.5Gbps, 2Gbps, even 5Gbps. But here's the catch: **standard Gigabit ethernet tops out at 1Gbps.**

If you're paying for speeds above 1Gbps but your router only has Gigabit ports, you're leaving bandwidth on the table. A 2.5G switch bridges that gap, giving you 2.5Gbps over standard Cat5e/Cat6 cable — no rewiring required.

**Use cases where 2.5G matters:**
- Sky's 5Gbps XGS-PON service
- Virgin Media Gig1 (1.1Gbps) and Gig2
- NAS backups and file transfers between devices
- Multiple 4K/8K streams without buffering
- Future-proofing for WiFi 6/7 access points

## Do You Actually Need 2.5G?

**Yes, if:**
- You have multi-gig broadband (1.5Gbps+) and want to use it
- You regularly transfer large files between devices
- You run a NAS and want faster backups
- You're adding WiFi 6/7 access points

**No, if:**
- Your internet is 1Gbps or under
- You only stream Netflix and browse
- Your devices are all Gigabit-only

## FAQ

### What does 2.5G mean?
2.5 Gigabits per second — 2.5x faster than standard Gigabit ethernet. It runs over the same Cat5e/Cat6 cables you probably already have.

### Will 2.5G work with my existing cables?
Yes. Cat5e handles 2.5G up to 100m. Cat6 is even better. No rewiring needed in most homes.

### Can I mix 2.5G and Gigabit devices?
Absolutely. 2.5G switches auto-negotiate. Gigabit devices connect at 1Gbps; 2.5G-capable devices connect at 2.5Gbps.

### Is PoE necessary?
Only if you're powering devices like WiFi access points or cameras over the same cable. Otherwise, a non-PoE 2.5G switch is cheaper.

### What's SFP+ and do I need it?
SFP+ is a slot for 10G fibre or copper modules. You don't *need* it, but it's useful for connecting to a NAS or server at full speed.
