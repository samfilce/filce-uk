## Why PoE Instead of WiFi?

Before we get into the picks: if you're serious about home security, PoE is the right call.

**WiFi cameras** are convenient for renters or low-stakes use, but they have real weaknesses: they depend on your router's WiFi coverage, they're susceptible to interference, and most cheap models have questionable cloud security. They also stop recording if your broadband goes down.

**PoE cameras** fix all of this. A single Cat5e or Cat6 cable carries both power and data. You can run them from a PoE NVR or a PoE switch connected to your home server. They work even if your internet is down, record locally to an NVR or NAS, and the image quality is consistently better because you're not compressing over WiFi.

If you're running [Home Assistant](/reviews/getting-started-home-assistant/), PoE cameras integrate via RTSP or ONVIF — no cloud dependency, no subscription required.

## What PoE Equipment Do You Need?

A PoE camera needs two things: a network connection and power. You have two ways to provide this:

**Option 1: PoE NVR (Network Video Recorder)**
A dedicated recorder with built-in PoE ports. Plug cameras straight in, and it handles storage, recording schedules, and playback. Simpler setup, less flexible. Good for pure CCTV use without Home Assistant.

**Option 2: PoE Switch + NAS/Server**
Run cameras into a PoE switch, stream to a NAS or home server running something like Frigate or Shinobi. More flexible, better Home Assistant integration, and you control everything locally. This is what I run.

## PoE Camera Buying Guide: What to Look For

### Resolution
4K (8MP) is the sweet spot in 2025. 2MP (1080p) is fine for indoor use but limiting outdoors. 4MP is a reasonable compromise if budget is tight.

### Night Vision
- **IR night vision:** Standard, works in complete darkness, black-and-white image
- **Colour night vision:** Needs some ambient light, but gives colour footage — much more useful for identification
- **Dual-light (spotlight):** Combines IR with a white light that activates on detection — best for active deterrence

### PoE Standard
Most home cameras use **802.3af** (15.4W). Higher-end cameras with PTZ or built-in heaters may need **802.3at** (30W). Check your PoE switch can supply what the camera needs.

### Home Assistant Compatibility
Any camera with **RTSP** or **ONVIF** support will work with Home Assistant. Reolink cameras have a native integration. Most other brands use ONVIF. Avoid cameras that only work via a proprietary cloud app.

## FAQ

### What does PoE stand for?
Power over Ethernet. It means the camera receives both its internet connection and electrical power through a single network cable (Cat5e or Cat6). No separate power supply required.

### Is Reolink worth it for UK buyers?
Yes. Reolink offers excellent value for money, solid Home Assistant compatibility, and decent build quality. For most home users, the RLC-810A or Fisheye PoE will outperform cameras costing twice as much from less well-known brands. We've tested the Fisheye PoE extensively — see our [full review](/posts/reolink-fisheye-camera-review/).

### Can I use PoE cameras without an NVR?
Yes. You can connect PoE cameras to a PoE switch and stream directly to a home server (running Frigate, Shinobi, or similar) or record to a NAS. Many Home Assistant users skip the NVR entirely and use Frigate for AI-powered detection.

### How many PoE cameras can I run?
As many as your PoE switch has ports. A standard 8-port PoE switch handles 8 cameras. Budget for total PoE wattage — most home cameras draw 7–12W each.

### Do PoE cameras work if my internet goes down?
Yes — this is one of the key advantages. PoE cameras connect to your local network and record to a local NVR or NAS. Internet connectivity is only needed if you want to view footage remotely.

### What cable do I need for PoE cameras?
Cat5e is the minimum, Cat6 is better. Maximum cable run is 100 metres. Ensure the cable is solid-core (not stranded) for permanent installations.

### Will PoE cameras work in cold UK winters?
Most PoE cameras are rated to -10°C or lower (check the spec sheet for each model). Hikvision and Amcrest models are generally rated for harsher conditions than budget alternatives.
